Salt & Churn Brand Design

tldr; Brand and identity design project for SVA’s Graphic Design class.

Tools Used: Adobe Illustrator, Adobe InDesign, Sketch.

In the fall of 2015 I took a Graphic Design class at the School of Visual Arts in New York City and our final project was brand design for a completely random company; we each picked a kind of company (i.e. bookstore, coffee shop, tutoring service etc.) and a taget audience

I got "an ice cream shop for cowboys".

We started with three possible mood boards, selected one, then refined it into a very clear brand direction, and ultimately produced a logo/wordmark and three pieces of branded content.

The real challenge was keeping it from being aggressively "portland-y" or "hipster-y".

This class was my first real push into design and for an introductory class, our teacher Jenny Kutnow (creative director at Etsy) gave us a lot of work and a lot of great feedback. I think what I admired most about her was how she taught by example of how to give constructive feedback on a designers work.

And how to not take feedback personally.

The class started with about 25 students but by the end of the semester we were a core group of about 12—at that point everyone was committing to their work and producing some really great designs.

Get it? 'Salt & Churn' is how you make ice cream!

After a lot of iterations I decided on the name Salt & Churn using Lucida Grande for the main text and Avenir for the body copy. I kept trying to introduce a few key brand colors, but nothing really stuck. I used them for the packaging to denote flavor, but I didn’t feel like they really served a purpose in the main wordmark.

From the research I did on modern day cowboys I decided to steer clear of the overly romanticized 'life on the prairie' stuff, or else my store would look too much like a hokey dive bar. Instead I wanted to focus textures like leather and wood and simple, paired-down flavors.

The inspiration for the ‘ice cream and jam’ pairing came from my uncle, who grew up on a farm and whose mother would always serve homemade ice cream with a spoonful of her strawberry preserves.

The menu and the sign came first—just setting the name onto different natural textures. I like to think that the name is literally branded onto the wooden sign.

The packing was the hardest and something I’m still not incredibly happy with. It was hard to find a free version of a blank ice cream package and I wasn’t that successful with making it look real. Maybe I’ll go back to it one day.
